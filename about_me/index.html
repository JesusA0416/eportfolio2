About Me and My Life

Hello. I’m Jesus Lopez Aguilar — not to be confused with the biblical one, though I have pulled off some last-minute miracles involving homework, Wi-Fi, and full-on panic.

I was born in Salt Lake City, Utah into a Mexican immigrant family. I didn’t apply for citizenship — I respawned here. People say if you’re born in the U.S., you “made it.” Cute. No one talks about the fine print: surprise taxes, translating government forms at 9, and learning how to file your parents’ W-2s before learning how to parallel park.

My culture isn’t unique — but I am. Mexican culture is shared: passed down through recipes, roasts, and generational trauma. But I’m the special seasoning — the unmeasured sprinkle of chaos that makes the dish hit different.

Want to know what’s actually unique? That one Mexican state where they eat roaches. Respectfully… you lost me at legs. Bugs? Absolutely not. A fly? Nasty. Shoo’d away with disgust. A bee? Immediate evacuation. A ladybug? No thanks. That’s a beetle with PR. A spider? If it’s visible, I scream. If it’s an inch or more, the lease is in its name. A tarantula? I'm not throwing a shoe — I’m throwing a remote and it's changing the channel on its own. If my mom screams at a rat, I scream too — not from fear, but from culturally trained solidarity.

You know the phrase: “I scream, you scream, we all scream for ice cream?” No. If you scream, I’m cracking my neck, spiritually detaching, and preparing to flee. My body doesn’t have a startle response. It has a full reboot.

When I say I’m different, I mean spiritually sensitive with a nervous system that thinks everything’s a threat. If someone speaks after 10 seconds of silence, I jump like I’ve been legally served. I’m not “on edge” — I am the edge.

I believe in the potato sock hack the way some people believe in astrology — with blind faith and zero accountability when their crush is a dog sign. I don’t care if it’s not scientifically proven — my abuela ran a trauma center from her kitchen and I’m still here. Fevers were cured with garlic, Vicks, and stubbornness. I didn’t grow up with checkups. I grew up with vaporub and a rosary.

I’m still using the same notebook from sophomore year. It deserves a diploma too. I have two mechanical pencils, and one is a limited edition from Japan that lives in a sacred pouch next to my rosary and whatever’s left of my dignity. Someone asked to borrow it once and said “can I keep it?” ¿De quién es? Mío. You say “era”? I’m tasing you. That pencil survived my emotional breakdowns — it’s family now.

Hispanic families love to brag. “My son got straight As!” “My daughter got into a great college!” That’s cute. I got a working washing machine and a pencil with a bow on it. Where’s my applause?

I loved tech, healthcare, criminal justice — but to everyone else, I’m just their Bluetooth technician, their bilingual blood pressure cuff, or apparently… immigration enforcement. Let me exist without a role, please.

And don’t touch the thermostat. We have two — but one is sacred. Touch it, and you’re on trial. “I’ve done this for years.” Yeah? I also filled out your tax returns and taught you how to use Google. Let’s talk.

I was once the kid staring at the new student. Then I transferred to Hillside Elementary and became that new kid. But it gave me friends — real ones — that I still talk to in 2025. We’re all Hispanic. We all believe in potato socks and trauma-based medicine. And we all made it.

Sometimes I spiral. “Para arriba, pa’ bajo, pa’ la izquierda, la derecha, el centro y pa’ dentro.” Not because I’m taking a shot — but because, for some reason, everyone’s chanting a biblical verse or calibrating their tequila like it’s a ritual. Meanwhile, I’m just trying to calibrate my emotional state because my Capri Sun straw went in sideways and now the entire pouch is leaking. Now I’m sticky, unstable, and I don’t even have a pouch to clink while someone says salud.
